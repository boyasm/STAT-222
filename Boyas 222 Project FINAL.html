Two protests in South Africaboth relating to trade worker factionalization spurred by dissatisfaction with the current ruling partyoccurred within two weeks of each other in Spring 2014. The protest in Johannesburg was a peaceful demonstration outside the High Court, complete with flags and signs. The protest in Christiana was a riot, complete with firebombs, lootingand a death. Why did the Christiana protest result in a death while the Johannesburg protest did not? I attempt to quantitatively analyze this question by examining data from social conflict events throughout the African continent during the period 19902011.

1. Data & Research Questions¶

Social conflictsdefined by political scientists Cullen S. Hendrix and Idean Salehyan as conflict events including, but not limited to, protests, riots, strikes, inter-communal conflict, and government violence against civiliansare differentiated from full scale intrastate war and mobilized conflict, but this differentiation does not make social conflict events less ubiquitous, disruptive, or dangerous (www.scaddata.org). First released in March 2011, the Social Conflict in Africa Database (SCAD) was prepared by Hendrix and Salehyan for the program on Climate Change and African Political Stability (CCAPS) at the Robert S. Strauss Center for International Security and Law at the University of Texas at Austin. The SCAD provides data on 7957 African social conflict events occurring in 48 countries between 1990 and 2011. Figure 1 and Table 1, below, attempt to give an overview of the conflicts included in the SCAD and some of the complexities therein.

I merged secondary datasets into the SCAD to add more information about country demographics. These datasets include the World Religions (WRP) and National Material Capabilities (NMC) datasets from the Correlates of War Project and the national freedom scores from the Polity IV Project. All datasets used in this project are available for direct download from the websites listed in section 7.2 at the end of this paper and in local cached form from the projects GitHub account (linked in section 7.1).

In this course, we reversed the traditional scientific process in that we started from data and then developed research questions. I started out the process with the general idea of investigating differences between social conflict events. After seeing my available data, I decided to focus on the following two main areas of inquiry and investigation:

  1. What differentiates an episode of social conflict that results in deaths from an episode of social conflict that does not result in deaths?

  2. Is there a way to predict the number of deaths that will result from an episode of social conflict?

2. Possible Impacts Of This Study¶

There is a relatively wide body of literature discussing African social conflict in the context of an overall process of political regime change. Although the body of literature on African conflict as a unique entity is almost nonexistent, there is a somewhat substantial theoretical and case study based literature about social conflict in general, not specific to the African continent (Scherrer 4). Scholars do not agree on a concise set of factors resulting in the escalation of social conflict events. Some researchers, such as peace scholar Christian Scherrer and anthropologist Jay OBrian, use ethnic and/or religious identity to explain differences in conflict severity. For example, O'Brian writes, Few of the nation-states created by Europe in Africa bore any relationship to any [natural ethnic or religious divides] (OBrian 63). On the contrary, geographer Adrian P. Wood argues, Shortages of natural resources lead to competition which may result in conflict (Wood 83). In contrast, sociologist Ralf Dahrendorf identifies economic disparities across a countrys social classes as a potentially major factor, and economist Massimo De Angelis expounds on this idea by giving an example from the United States: The Great Depression, with its historically high levels of unemployment, did not make the American working class more docile. On the contrary, it sparked open insurrection: Dont starvefight! was one slogan (Dahrendorf 52; De Angelis 51). As sociologist Nigel Fielding notes, conflicts can be attributed to any, some, or all of class, ethnicity, gender and sexual politics, region, nation, employment status, age, and ideology (Fielding 5). There is no scholarly consensus on how to explain the escalation of a conflict.

Since the release of the SCAD dataset in 2011, a handful of quantitative studies specifically focusing on African conflicts have used the SCAD as a primary source. These quantitative studies, like literature referenced earlier, identify a variety of factors influencing conflict escalation ranging from climate change to food price spikes to the occurrence of elections (Devlin, Franck, & Hendrix; Smith; Salehyan & Linebarger). However, data on external variables such as weather patterns, food prices, and election schedules are not part of the SCAD, and a unifying theme between these studies is a lack of reproducibility. Most of the papers are published without code and, in many cases, without links to specific datasets used for analysis, making it impossible to replicate or further investigate their results.

Therefore, a reproducibly transparent quantitative exploration of the factors that precede the escalation of African social conflict events would be a welcome addition to the academic debate. Furthermore, if this analysis were to provide substantial evidence in the form of a predictive model for a set of factors leading to death, such a model would be of great use to the aidworker community seeking to prevent conflicts from escalating and save lives.

3. Data Cleaning & Manipulation¶

All conflicts in the SCAD dataset are included in this study, with the exception of 119 conflicts that do not start and end in the same calendar year. Such conflicts were excluded from the analysis due to the complexities of merging secondary data sources delineated by calendar year onto multiyear conflicts; these multiyear conflicts represent under 1.5 percent of the entire dataset, so removing them from the study will have little or no impact on the results. The NMC and Polity IV datasets provide information on a countryyear level, and variables such as democracy scores, iron production, and total country population were directly merged into the SCAD. The World Religion dataset measures the number of people who subscribe to a specific religion at five-year intervals for every country. Consequently, I computed the dominant religion in every county per five years, forward filled the data to create country-year measurements, and merged onto SCAD. I used the rest of the variables with little manipulation, except creating a death/no death indicator.

4. Methods & Results¶

The work described here approaches the two research questions sequentially. I first conduct a broad descriptive analysis of a variety of factors that could potentially influence a conflicts number of deaths. I then proceed to the modeling phase of the project, breaking the modeling task into two subtasks: (1) attempt to model the death/no death indicator variable, and (2) use the results of that previous modeling effort and attempt to model the actual number of deaths.

Before investigating any of the research questions, I attempt to get a sense for the distribution of the number of deaths. 4.1 Differences Between Death and No Death Conflicts¶

Now that I have discussed the overall distribution of the data, I will do some exploratory analyses across some of the variables scholars suggest might make a death more likely. First, I analyze magnitude variables such as duration of the conflict and the number of participants; one might hypothesize that the larger a conflict in duration or in number of participants, the more deaths. Figures 3 and 4, below, show that this intuition is, in fact, not supported by the data.

It follows that there should be an increasing relationship between these variables, as the longer a conflict, the more chances for a death to result. However, the plot does not support such a conclusion; considering the fact that we have many more shorter conflicts in the dataset, there does not seem to be that much of a relationship between duration and the number of deaths.

Now consider the dominant religion in a particular country. As recounted in the literature review portion of this paper, scholars such as Christian Scherrer suggest that conflicts can escalate along ethnic divides, so one could hypothesize that countries with a particularly dominant religion might sustain more conflicts resulting in death. Once again, Table 2 illustrates that this hypothesis is not supported by the dataset. The distributions across religion look essentially the same across death and no death conflicts. However, running a chi-square goodness of fit test on the contingency table yields a significant result (p=2.86e-7, X2=33.24, df=3). This significance appears to relate to the large difference for Animist-dominated countries; since Animist-dominated countries constitute a very small percentage of the sample, it seems that this significant test could be an anomaly of sampling.

One could hypothesize, then, that country boundaries could differentiate between conflicts with and without deaths. The SCAD provides latitude and longitude data for each conflict, so assessing this proposition is relatively straightforward. In a last effort to find a variable that impacts the frequency of deaths, I will analyze the idea suggested by scholars such as political scientist F. Ugboaja Ohaegbulam that countries of a different regime type could encourage conflicts to escalate to different extents (Ohaegbulam 116). One might suppose that strong democracies would encourage free speech and therefore be more inclined to foster peaceful social conflict, while oppressive governments might violently stamp out conflicts when they first begin. Table 3 shows the frequency of death and no death conflicts split by regime type, and a pattern emerges in the data, though not exactly the in accord with the original hypothesis. Regime type is measured by the Freedom House Polity score which ranges as integers from -10 (full autocracy) to +10 (full democracy).

The above table seems to show that democracies, particularly weak democracies, have more death-resulting conflicts than do autocracies. This counterintuitive observation could potentially be explained by the idea that democracy fosters a political environment where citizens can speak their minds, which can lead to more situations of death.

In fact, as can be easily seen by the row percentages printed in the table cells, the data actually suggest that democracies are more likely to result in death conflicts and autocracies no death. A chi square goodness of fit test on the contingency table supports the visual conclusion that the distributions are not the same (p=8.89e-27, X2=128.32, df=4). Why could this occur? Perhaps an autocratic government are more likely to discourage violent dissent because citizens know the government will retaliate if things get out of hand. The opposite argument holds for democracies. It is also certainly possible that this counterintuitive pattern is somewhat meaningless, either a false pattern in otherwise random data or a pattern that is a symptom of another, unanalyzed variable. In any case, this is an interesting result that I will investigate further in the modeling efforts of this project. All in all, individual variables do not seem to offer a lot of insight into factors that can cause a conflict to be deadly.

4.2 Modeling¶

Although the exploration of individual variables had limited success, I will now move on to the modeling phase of this project. I randomly split the data into training and testing sets (70-30 percent, respectively), and first try to model the death/no death indicator variable before moving on to modeling the more complicated absolute number of deaths in a given conflict. Both modeling efforts employ similar modeling techniques. I first use regression techniques to build a model, selecting a set of predictors. Then I use that selected subset of variables to train models using other methods in an effort to assess model robustness.

4.2.1 Death/No Death Indicator¶

Using the training set, I start by running logistic regression on the indicator death/no death variable. I select an initial set of variables based on the literature and exploratory analyses conducted previously in section 4.1, transforming any categorical variables into dummies. I use a modified best subset technique to do model selection by making experimental adjustments to the variables included in the model and choosing the model with the smallest prediction residual sum of squares. I recognize that this is a somewhat rough variable selection process and will discuss potential limitations and improvements later in the paper (section 6). The following five variables were chosen to be included in the final model:

  • Location (categorical)where was the conflict located within a countrys borders?
  • Event Type (categorical)organized/spontaneous, riot/strike/protest, etc.
  • Was the Central Government a Target? (0/1)
  • Primary Issue Motivating the Conflict (categorical)
  • Composite Index of National Capability (CINC) score (numeric)attempts to represent a countrys hard power

Now I train a k-nearest neighbor model (KNN) using the same set of variables, setting k=10 through a similar rough trial-and-error process (again, limitations and improvements to this process will be discussed in section 6).

With some success in modeling the death/no death indicator variable, I now move on to attempt to model the absolute number of deaths in a given conflict. I use the same training and testing sets as in Section 4.2.1 and apply a similar variable selection process using Poisson regression. Interestingly, the exact same set of five variables minimized prediction error for this modeling effort as did for modeling the death/no death indicator, perhaps lending more support for the importance of those variables. With variables selected, I again train other models using different techniques to test robustness, this time a classification/regression tree (CART) in addition to a KNN (k=3).

Figure 7 shows the Fitted Value vs. Residual plots for predicting the number of deaths using the same set of variables from the same test dataset and three different modeling techniques: Poisson Regression, KNN (K=3), and CART Decision Tree. Poisson regression and KNN both seem to overfit, while CART seems to do a very good job predicting throughout. The two largest residuals across all three methods are the same conflicts, with the third largest residual for Poisson and KNN also the same conflict.

Figure 7, above, shows the results of these three models by plotting the natural log of fitted values (with an unlogged reference axis on top) against the size of the residual. The Poisson Regression and KNN models appear to be similar, with a decent amount of spread in the residuals and a tendency to overpredict as the fitted values get large. The KNN has a large spike of residuals for conflicts that have a fitted value of 0 deaths; this could be an argument for the KNN model being the least successful of the three, as the model seems very inaccurate at predicting conflicts with no deaths. The decision tree, by contrast to the other two methods, seems to have residuals closely centered around zero with very little overarching pattern, suggesting that the tree could be a very successful model; in fact, its residual sum of prediction squares is far lower than that of the other two models.

However, all three methods have some very large residuals in the thousands, and exploration of those residuals shows that they are, in fact, the same set of conflicts across all three models (denoted in color by the legend in Figure 7). Investigation into these particular conflicts does not yield any revelations into why they might be consistently poorly predicted. The data for the conflicts does not seem unreasonable, so I am unwilling to remove them from the analyses. While the decision tree seems to do an exceptional job at predicting on the test set, the presence of these outliers without a clear explanation as to why raises some concerns that something could be missing from the model. More work needs to be done investigating these conflicts. Perhaps the important information about what causes death can be gleaned from these outlier conflicts that do not behave like the rest.

5. Discussion¶

Overall, the analyses up until this point seem to be somewhat successful. I built a set of models that rely on the same five variableslocation, event type, central government target, primary issue, and CINC scoreand seem relatively robust across methods at predicting on the test set. Notably, the variable that seemed to have an impact during the first portion of this study, the Polity IV score for regime type, was not included in the variable selection process. I had previously noted that the patterns highlighted in the analysis of regime type were counterintuitive to preconceived expectations and had wondered if the variable was truly a good predictor of deaths in a conflict (Section 4.1). The fact that the regime type variable was not selected in either modeling effort suggests that perhaps the skepticism in exploratory analyses was warranted.

Of more note than what was omitted is what variables were selected into the model. The five selected variables do not come from any single school of scholarship. The CINC score focuses on factors strictly related to the countrys government, both related to natural resources and government policy. The other four variables focus on the conflict itself, but they focus on different subareas of interest: geography, social concerns spurring the conflict, and conflict logistics. In short, my analysis here does not support any one particular scholars opinion but instead supports the heated debate. Conflict escalation seems to be as complex as the scholarship might suggesta product of multiple factors that measure different aspects of the sociopolitical environment surrounding a conflict.

6. Future Extensions and Final Conclusions¶

While the conclusions in this study show promise with respect to understanding social conflict in Africa, more work needs to be done. On one hand, future analyses should try to merge or generate more variables to better assess some of the existing hypotheses surrounding social conflict escalation. As described by the literature review in Section 2, some of these variables should be representations of ethnic groups within a country, weather patterns, election data, and historical food prices. On the other hand, the actual statistical analyses conducted hereparticularly concerning variable selection and model testingare rough at best. I conducted analyses on this project before learning more advanced modeling techniques such as stepwise variable selection, the lasso, and an in-depth discussion of cross validation. The original project proposal did not specifically include these techniques, but it would be much better to re-run these analyses using a more organized variable selection process such as stepwise selection and a more accurate model assessment process such as cross-validation. Making such changes would allow me to have much more confidence in the usefulness and robustness of the results.

Limitations withstanding, this analysis represents a useful reproducible quantitative analysis of African social conflict. In many ways, these conclusions support the variety of theoretical hypotheses surrounding conflict escalation: a combination of varied factors seems to impact the number of deaths that will result from an episode of social conflict. We have the beginnings of a successful model that, with some refinement, could make an impact on both the academic and international aid-worker communities. Finally, perhaps more important than the specific conclusions about the conflicts is the research process itself. In stark contrast to previous studies using the SCAD, this study has its entire research workflowcode, plots, datasets, documentation, etc.available on GitHub for other students and researchers to explore, utilize, and extend. Such a transparent and reproducible workflow lays the groundwork for future work and is an important first step towards regularly using quantitative analyses to analyze sociopolitical issues such as social conflict in Africa.
